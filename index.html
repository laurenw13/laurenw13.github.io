Tutorial: Analyzing Adverse Events Associated with COVID-19 Vaccines¶

Lauren Wei¶

Motivation¶

Since the beginning of 2020 when COVID-19 started becoming a widespread disease around the world, the lives of many have been greatly impacted. COVID-19 not only impaired our mental and physical health, but also destructed the economy and affected the livings of many people. After the world has been combating COVID-19 for about a year, the emergence of COVID-19 vaccines seem like a ray of hope for stopping the disease. According to the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), studies have shown the effectivenss of mRNA vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna) to be 94%. While many people are ready to get vaccinated, some people are more reserved as they worry about the possible side effects associated with the vaccines. As there have been reports about the adverse events associated with COVID-19 vaccines, I think this would be a great topic worth exploring. The purpose of the analysis is to allow the readers to better understand the risks associated with the vaccines and to possibly identify the groups of people who are more prone to the adverse side effects. This tutorial is going to walk you through the data science pipeline to perform such analysis.

Disclaimer: This tutorial is in no way discouraging getting vaccinationed for COVID-19 as many studies have proven the benefits of getting vaccinated. The purpose of this analysis is only to provide a better understanding of the adverse events associated with the COVID-19 vaccines. learn more about COVID-19 vaccines: https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/vaccines/index.html

1. Data Collection¶

About the data¶

The data for this tutorial is retrieved from the Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System (VAERS), which is cosponsored by the CDC and the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). https://vaers.hhs.gov/data/datasets.html. The system receives reports of adverse events associated with vaccines from doctors and vaccine providers. An important note about the data is that "no cause and effect relationship has been established for any reported event." Therefore, the data should only be providing us with possible correlations. The data provides reports of individuals who experienced adverse events associated with vaccines in 2021. To read more about the data use guide: https://vaers.hhs.gov/docs/VAERSDataUseGuide_November2020.pdf

2. Data Management¶

2.1 Dealing with duplicates¶

Each patient is only identified once by VAERS ID in the dataframe that contains patients's information. However, the other dataframes contain duplicates of VAERS ID representing different doses of vaccines and multiple symptoms of the same patient. Hence, I will remove duplicates in the vaccine dataframe and combine the symptoms of the same patient into one entry in the symptoms dataframe.

In [553]:
# drop duplicates in the vaccines dataframe

symptoms_by_patient.columns = ['VAERS_ID', 'SYMPTOMS']

3. Exploratory data analysis¶

3.1 Look for common symptoms¶

Since there are many possible symptoms from adverse events associated with the vaccine, we want to look for the common symptoms and see the percentage for each. Common symptoms here are defined as symptoms with a frequency of more than 1% out of all the possible symptoms.

From the plot we can see that headache is the most common symptom, with a frequency of 4.16%, followed by pyrexia with 3.53%, and then chills with 3.14%. We can also see that death as a result of adverse events has a frequency of 1.10%. However, it is noteworthy that this is out of all adverse events reported but not out of all vaccinated individual. From the distribution of the different symptoms, we can see that besides the four leading common symptoms, types of symptoms seem to be pretty spread out. Common symptoms have frequency around 1 to 4 percent, while most of the symptoms have frequency below 1%.

3.2 Associations with sex and age groups¶

The next thing that we are going to explore is the association between adverse events and sex and age groups. Sex and age groups are two considerably common classifications in the medical world, and hence might be helpful when searching for patterns. Here, we are going to classify age groups as Child (0-14), Young Adult (15-47), Mid Age (48-63), and Elder (>63). The classifications are based on a study linked here https://bmcneurol.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12883-020-01888-w

From the pie chart based on age groups, we can see that 45.39% of patients are young adults. This is quite surprising, as many might expect older individuals to be more prone to adverse events. One possible explanation might be that young adults have stronger immune system and hence react more strongly to vaccines. Another possible explanation is that young adults make up a large portion of vaccinated individuals and hence resulted in more adverse events. Elders and mid age individuals make up about the same percentage, one with 26.81% and the other with 27.63%. From the pie chart based on sex, we can tell that females take up a significantly larger portion with 72.79%. This might suggest that females are more prone to adverse events associated with the COVID-19 vaccine.

4. Hypothesis testing and machine learning¶

4.1 Linear Regression¶

From the exploratory data analysis, we observed that sex, age groups, and time seem to have effects on the number of patients with adverse events. Here I form the hypothesis that sex, age groups, and time have linear relationships with the number of patients. We will set the significance level to be 0.05. We are going to build and train a linear regression model using these three terms to test our hypothesis. We choose to use linear regression here because we want to find a simple and general trend for the relationship. While a polynomial regression might result in better predictive power, it is also more prone to overfitting.

From our hypothesis, we know that the null hypothesis states that the parameters have no relationship. Based on the t-test result, we can see that the p-values for ONSET_DATE and Young Adult are all below 0.05. Since we set the significance level to be 0.05, we would hence reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the parameters ONSET_DATE (which represent time) and Young Adult have a linear relationship with the dependent variable, which is the number of patients. However, the p-values for Mid Age, Elder, and M (male) are all greater than 0.05. We will not reject the null hypothesis and thus cannot conclude that they have a linear relationship with the number of patients. By predicting using the training set and the testing set, we can see that they have similar accuracy, which shows that the model does not overfit. However, the accuracy is only around 0.50, which does not provide a good predictive power.

4.2 Linear Regression with Interaction Terms¶

Our previous attempt on looking for a linear regression was not very successive. So let's try a possible way that might improve our model. From the 'Number of patients conditioned on time' graph that we created earlier, we can see that it seems like age groups have an effect on changing the trend of the the dependent variable. Hence, adding interaction terms for age groups and time might be able to make the model better.
